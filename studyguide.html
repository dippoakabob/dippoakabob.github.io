Study Guide

The test will be held in-class on Thursday November 5 and will be open-note; only hand-written notes may be used during the test – no printouts or screens.

Format: The exam will be the same format as Test 1 and consist of matching, multiple choice, short answer, and short essay items.  Test materials will be provided for you, you do not need to purchase blue books or anything like that.

Content covered on the test:

1. You are responsible for the following assigned films: Closely Watched Trains, Daisies, 4 Months 3 Weeks 2 Days, and Kontroll. Be sure to have directors, setting (historical and geographical), and major themes we’ve talked about in class in your notes. Be able to describe distinctive cinematic features of the films and the basic plot of the films. Keep these questions in mind: what city or country is the film set in? what social issues are raised? what time period is in focus? is a particular historical event depicted? what major cinematic techniques are used in the film in important ways? how are these films representative of their respective “waves”?

2. You should be able to define the following cinematic terms: color, contrast, color grading, color association, color transition, auteurism. You should also have 2-3 examples of which colors are generally linked with which film genres and with which emotions, why "blue and orange" might be becoming such dominant colors in Hollywood films, the three key elements to any color and a brief definition of each, and one or two ways color association is created in a film. (Review the videos from the Oct 22 homework assignment and the Yale Film Analysis Guide if you are unsure of any of the above). Cinematic terms from the Part 1 (Russia / USSR) of the course are fair game for Test 2 as well; be sure to have terms listed in the study guide for Test 1 in your notes if you did poorly on that section on the last test.

3. You should have a rough sense of the history of Czechoslovakia, Romania, and Hungary.  You should be able to identify the basic pattern in Eastern European history in the 20th century.

4. You should be able to place the countries, regions, and select cities we have noted in this part of the course (Russia, Czechoslovakia, Romania, Hungary) on a map of Europe.

5. You should know who the people, places, events, etc. listed below are and why they are important the history and cinematic history of Czechoslovakia, Romania, and Hungary; review the powerpoints on each country on d2l and check Wikipedia or Google them if you need further details for your notes. Know the following people and events: 1989, Reinhard Heydrich, Jan Palach, Vaclav Havel, FAMU, Barrandov Studios, Milos Forman, Jiri Menzel, Vera Chytilova, Jan Svankmajer, Bohumil Hrabal, Prague Spring, Velvet Revolution, The Breakup of Czechoslovakia, Cristian Mungiu, Nicolae Ceausescu, Vlad Tepes, Securitate, East Germany, The Berlin Wall, Ostalgie, Bela Kun, 1956 Hungarian Revolution, Imre Nagy, Victor Orban, Fidesz, Nimrod Antal, Istvan Szabo, The Warsaw Pact.

6. Films from student-group presentations may appear in connection with questions about countries of origin, major themes, or cinematic “waves” they fit.

7. You should be able to identify defining features of the Czech New Wave and the Romanian New Wave and list one or two major directors associated with them. You should be able to discuss how Czech New Wave differed from Socialist Realism.

8.  You will not be asked any questions about the Russian / Soviet films we watched in Part 1 of the course, nor will you need to identify any Russian/Soviet directors or events from Russian/Soviet history.
